Thermal Isolation and Safety Design Study: Exhaust Mounting Solution (ANSYS FEA)

Summary: To design and validate a safe, high-efficiency mounting solution for a 190°C diesel heater exhaust pipe, focusing on minimizing heat conduction to the surrounding structure in a cold 0 °C environment.

I utilized SolidWorks to model the plate, which served as a substitute for a jalousie window panel. This model was then simulated under conditions of an exhaust pipe running through it, based on the four materials I had readily available, to determine the best material for this application.

Methodology: CAD & Steady-State Thermal FEA

The entire analysis followed a rigorous digital engineering workflow to ensure accuracy and inform the final design recommendation.

  1. CAD Modeling (SolidWorks): The plate geometry was precisely modeled in SolidWorks, including the critical central hole for the exhaust pipe.
  2. Analysis Setup (ANSYS Discovery): The model was imported for a Steady-State Thermal FEA to evaluate heat conduction across materials.
  3. Defining Boundary Conditions: The simulation mimicked a worst-case scenario:
    • Heat Source (Input): A 190°C Fixed Temperature was applied to the inner face of the exhaust hole, simulating the hot pipe surface.
    • Cooling Environment (Output): Convection was applied to all exterior surfaces h = 10 W/(m·K ) at 0 °C, representing cold ambient conditions.
  4. Material Comparison: Four simulations were run, one for each material (Carbon Steel, Stainless Steel, Aluminum, and Plywood), using their distinct Thermal Conductivity (k) values.
  5. Key Metric: The Total Heat Flux (Qout) was measured to quantify the total power (in Watts) escaping the plate, determining the most effective thermal isolation.

Results and Conclusion

The analysis compared materials with widely varying thermal conductivity, leading to a clear conclusion regarding performance and non-combustible safety requirements.

Material Tested Thermal Conductivity (k) Total Heat Flow (Qout) Performance Summary
Carbon Steel, 1020 54.1 W/(m·K) 39.3 W High Conduction. Baseline material; poor thermal isolation.
Stainless Steel, 316 14.6 W/(m·K) 20.6 W Practical Thermal Barrier. Reduces heat flow by 47.6% compared to Carbon Steel.
Aluminum alloy, 6061-T6 155 W/(m·K) 53.5 W Highest Conduction. Highest heat loss, offering the least thermal isolation. Qout.
Plywood 0.13 W/(m·K) 0.856 W Optimal Heat Isolation. Lowest heat loss, providing superior thermal isolation. Qout.

Final Engineering Recommendation:

The analysis confirmed that Plywood delivered the superior thermal performance (Qout) = 0.856 W for thermal isolation. While initially flagged due to the fire safety hazard posed by the 190°C heat source, the final engineering decision is to select Plywood and mitigate the flammability risk by adding a non-combustible heat shield or ceramic collar between the exhaust pipe and the wood surface.

This approach prioritizes the optimal thermal performance of the wood, resulting in a 97.8% reduction in heat flow compared to the Carbon Steel baseline, and demonstrates the ability to solve a safety constraint through design iteration rather than performance compromise.

Structural Integrity and Rigidity Study: E36 Performance Clutch Fork (SolidWorks Simulation FEA)

Summary: To design and validate a high-strength replacement eyelet for the BMW E36 clutch pedal assembly. This project addresses the notorious failure point of the factory plastic eyelet, which is prone to cracking and complete detachment when paired with high-pressure aftermarket performance clutches.

I utilized SolidWorks to model a custom-engineered metal eyelet as a robust, cost-effective alternative to expensive aftermarket conversion kits. By simulating the component under a "worst-case" 2500 N panic-load, I verified that this design provides a permanent fix to the "dead pedal" failure mode while improving shift precision through enhanced rigidity.

Design Intent & Value Engineering

The OEM eyelet is made of brittle plastic, which often fails under the increased hydraulic resistance of performance clutch systems. While boutique billet conversion kits exist, they can be prohibitively expensive. This study demonstrates a "Value Engineering" approach: designing a precision-machined metal eyelet that delivers professional-grade reliability and a 9.31 Factor of Safety at a fraction of the market cost.

Methodology: CAD & Static Structural FEA

The analysis followed a rigorous engineering workflow to ensure the part could handle the significant mechanical advantage generated by the clutch pedal lever arm.

  1. CAD Modeling (SolidWorks): The eyelet was modeled to match the critical dimensions of the E36 pedal pin and the Tilton-style master cylinder rod, replacing plastic with high-strength structural metal.
  2. Analysis Setup (SolidWorks Simulation): A Static Structural study was conducted to evaluate the eyelet’s response to high-leverage axial forces.
  3. Defining Boundary Conditions:
    • Load (Input): A 2500 N (~560 lbs) axial force was applied. This accounts for the 4:1 leverage ratio of the pedal and the high-clamp pressure of a Stage 2 hydraulic system during rapid engagement.
    • Fixtures (Constraints): The eyelet pin-hole was fixed to simulate the pivot point on the clutch pedal arm.
  4. Sectional Analysis: Used Section Clipping to verify internal stress distribution, ensuring the transition between the threaded shaft and the eyelet body was structurally sound.
  5. Key Metrics: Von Mises Stress was checked against material yield, and Resultant Displacement was recorded to ensure a direct, non-compliant pedal feel.
