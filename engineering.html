Thermal Isolation & Safety Design Study

Exhaust Mounting Solution — Steady-State Thermal FEA

ANSYS Discovery SolidWorks Thermal FEA Material Science

Objective

Design and validate a safe, high-efficiency mounting solution for a 190°C diesel heater exhaust pipe passing through a structural panel in a 0°C ambient environment. The goal was to minimize heat conduction to the surrounding structure while maintaining non-combustible safety requirements.

Methodology

The analysis followed a rigorous digital engineering workflow using CAD modeling in SolidWorks followed by Steady-State Thermal FEA in ANSYS Discovery. Four materials with widely varying thermal conductivity were compared across identical boundary conditions.

  • CAD plate geometry modeled in SolidWorks with a central exhaust hole
  • 190°C Fixed Temperature applied to inner face of exhaust hole (heat source)
  • Convection (h = 10 W/m·K) applied to all exterior surfaces at 0°C ambient
  • Total Heat Flux (Qout) measured as the key performance metric across all four material simulations

Simulation Results

Quantitative Results

Material Thermal Conductivity (k) Total Heat Flow (Qout) Performance
Carbon Steel 1020 54.1 W/(m·K) 39.3 W Baseline — poor isolation
Stainless Steel 316 14.6 W/(m·K) 20.6 W 47.6% reduction vs. carbon steel
Aluminum 6061-T6 155 W/(m·K) 53.5 W Highest conduction — worst isolation
Plywood 0.13 W/(m·K) 0.856 W 97.8% reduction — optimal isolation

Engineering Recommendation

Plywood delivered optimal thermal performance (Qout = 0.856 W) — a 97.8% reduction versus carbon steel baseline. Rather than eliminating plywood due to the 190°C fire hazard, the final design decision adds a non-combustible ceramic collar between the exhaust pipe and wood surface. This approach solves the safety constraint through design iteration rather than performance compromise.

Structural Integrity Study: E36 Clutch Fork Eyelet

Performance Clutch Upgrade — Static Structural FEA & Value Engineering

SolidWorks Simulation Static Structural FEA Von Mises Stress Value Engineering

Objective

Design and validate a high-strength metal replacement eyelet for the BMW E36 clutch pedal assembly. The factory plastic eyelet is prone to brittle fracture under the increased hydraulic load of aftermarket performance clutches — a failure mode that leaves the driver with a dead pedal. This study validates a precision-machined metal alternative at a fraction of the boutique aftermarket cost.

Methodology

Static Structural FEA was conducted in SolidWorks Simulation under a 2500 N worst-case panic-load, accounting for the 4:1 mechanical advantage of the clutch pedal arm and the high clamp pressure of a Stage 2 hydraulic system.

  • Custom eyelet modeled in SolidWorks to match E36 pedal pin and Tilton-style master cylinder rod dimensions
  • 2500 N (~560 lbs) axial force applied at clutch pedal leverage ratio
  • Eyelet pin-hole fixed as pivot constraint
  • Section clipping used to verify internal stress at the threaded shaft–eyelet body transition
  • Von Mises Stress and Resultant Displacement recorded as key metrics

Bolt Stress Testing & FEA Analysis

Multi-Material Bolt Selection Study · ANSYS FEA

ANSYS FEA Mesh Convergence Apr – May 2023

Overview

Assessed bolt sizes and materials using ANSYS to determine optimal bolt selection for various loading applications. The study evaluated stress resistance and failure thresholds across multiple bolt configurations under compressive, shear, and combined loading conditions.

Methodology

  • Performed finite element analysis to simulate compressive, shear, and combined loading on bolted plate assemblies
  • Identified critical structural failure modes and stress concentrations for each configuration
  • Conducted mesh refinement and convergence studies to validate model accuracy
  • Evaluated stress, deformation, and safety factors across varying load cases
